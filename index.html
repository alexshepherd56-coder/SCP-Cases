30.1 Microcytic Anaemia (Haematology)

HaematologyMedicineGeneral Practice

Case Summary

Gabriella Pesce, 24, presents with worsening fatigue. She has adopted a vegetarian diet to control weight and appears pale (family also concerned). You suspect anaemia related to diet.

Questions & Answers

Q1. What are the key features of history and examination in your assessment of Gabriella and why?
  • Features of anaemia: SOB, fatigue, pallor.
  • Iron deficiency stigmata: glossitis, angular stomatitis, koilonychia, Plummer–Vinson dysphagia, brittle hair, pica, achlorhydria.
  • Bleeding/menstrual history and diet review (vegetarian/low iron intake).
fileciteturn0file1
Q2. What is anaemia?
  • Reduced haemoglobin: <135 g/L (adult male), <115 g/L (adult female).
  • Not a disease itself—reflects underlying process; may be dilutional (e.g., pregnancy).
  • Classify by MCV: microcytic, macrocytic, normocytic; or by mechanism.
fileciteturn0file1
Q3. Why does lack of iron cause anaemia?
  • Depletion sequence: marrow iron stores ↓ (ferritin ↓) → serum iron ↓ & TIBC ↑ (latent) → impaired Hb synthesis → overt anaemia.
  • Later, iron lost from liver/skin/muscle.
fileciteturn0file1
Q4. Microcytosis with low Hb: causes and how iron studies help?
  • Causes: iron deficiency (commonest), sideroblastic anaemia, thalassaemia, anaemia of chronic disease, lead poisoning.
  • Blood film: hypochromic microcytosis; target cells/basophilic stippling more in haemoglobinopathies.
  • Distinguish IDA vs thalassaemia: ethnic background; RBC count vs Hb; RDW ↑ in IDA, normal in thalassaemia.
fileciteturn0file1
Q5. Outline your plans to investigate Gabriella’s anaemia.
  • FBC with indices, blood film, reticulocytes.
  • Iron studies: ferritin, serum iron, transferrin/TIBC, TSAT.
  • Consider coeliac screen, menstrual/GI blood loss workup; dietary review.
fileciteturn0file1
Q6. Explain iron absorption and metabolism relevant to management.
  • Absorption mainly in duodenum/jejunum; enhanced by acidity and vitamin C; inhibited by phytates/tannins.
  • Transported on transferrin; stored as ferritin/haemosiderin; regulated by hepcidin.
  • Dietary optimisation + oral iron replacement.

30.2 Macrocytic Anaemia (Haematology)

HaematologyMedicineGeneral Practice

Case Summary

Maude McArthur, 75, presents with a sore, inflamed tongue and angular cheilitis. Diet recently reduced to tea and toast. You suspect nutritional deficiency causing macrocytic anaemia.

Questions & Answers

Q1. Key features of history and examination and why?
  • Dietary intake (green leafy veg), dentition, home/cooking situation, alcohol.
  • Autoimmune risks for pernicious anaemia (other autoimmune disease, FHx).
  • Examination for severe anaemia and neurology (B12: peripheral neuropathy, subacute combined degeneration, optic/mental changes).
fileciteturn0file2
Q2. Clinical findings suggesting B12 and/or folate deficiency?
  • Skin/oral: angular cheilosis, beefy red glossitis, pigmentation, alopecia.
  • GI: anorexia, weight loss, malabsorption.
  • CNS: dementia, peripheral neuropathy, SACD (gait, paraesthesiae, weakness), optic atrophy.
fileciteturn0file2
Q3. Role of Vitamin B12 and folate in red cell metabolism.
  • Co-factors for DNA synthesis/repair/methylation; essential in rapidly dividing cells (erythropoiesis).
fileciteturn0file2
Q4. Why macrocytosis with B12/folate deficiency?
  • Nuclear–cytoplasmic asynchrony: DNA synthesis impaired; RNA/protein relatively preserved → cytoplasmic enlargement with delayed mitosis → large oval RBCs.
fileciteturn0file2
Q5. Pernicious anaemia: mechanism and B12 absorption overview.
  • B12 from animal foods; requires gastric acid and intrinsic factor for absorption in terminal ileum.
  • Pernicious anaemia: autoimmune loss of IF/parietal cells → malabsorption → B12 deficiency despite intake.
  • Liver stores for years; daily requirement ~3–5 μg.
fileciteturn0file2
Q6. Investigations for suspected pernicious anaemia.
  • FBC/film (macro-ovalocytes), B12, folate, reticulocytes, LDH, bilirubin.
  • Intrinsic factor/parietal cell antibodies; consider coeliac/autoimmune screen.
  • Assess for neurological involvement.

30.3 Aplastic Anaemia (SDL) (Haematology)

HaematologyMedicineHospital Ward

Case Summary

Julian Barker, 25, has persistent oral thrush and gum bleeds. FBC: anaemia with low neutrophils and platelets. Provisional diagnosis: aplastic anaemia.

Questions & Answers

Q1. What is aplastic anaemia?
  • Pancytopenia (anaemia, thrombocytopenia, neutropenia) with hypocellular/acellular marrow; >70% idiopathic; remainder acquired/congenital.
fileciteturn0file3
Q2. Key features of history/examination and why?
  • Identify causes: drugs, viral infections (e.g., hepatitis), rheumatologic/immune, pregnancy.
  • Assess for marrow failure triad: anaemia (fatigue), bleeding (mucocutaneous), infection (neutropenia).
fileciteturn0file3
Q3. Clinical features and underlying pathophysiology?
  • Immune-mediated suppression of haematopoiesis (T-cell mediated); stem-cell defect likely.
  • Features: anaemia, bleeding, bacterial infections; hepatosplenomegaly/lymphadenopathy uncommon (suggests alternate dx).
fileciteturn0file3
Q4. Poor prognostic investigation findings?
  • Diagnostic criteria (peripheral + marrow) and poor prognosis when ≥2 of: ANC <0.5×10⁹/L; platelets <20×10⁹/L; reticulocytes <40×10⁹/L.
fileciteturn0file3
Q5. Why protective isolation and how implemented on the ward?
  • Protect patient from Multi-Resistant Organisms due to neutropenia: PPE station; signage; daily first clean; aseptic technique for lines/dressings; frequent surface cleaning.
fileciteturn0file3
Q6. Principles of supportive care in marrow failure.
  • Transfuse RBCs/platelets as needed; prompt antibiotics for infection; growth factors/definitive therapy per specialist.
fileciteturn0file3
Q7. ABO/Rh meaning of “B positive”.
  • ABO type B antigen present; anti-A in plasma; Rh(D) antigen present.
fileciteturn0file3
Q8. Immunological and non-immunological transfusion complications (for consent).
  • Immunological: acute/delayed haemolytic, febrile non-haemolytic, allergic/anaphylaxis, TRALI, alloimmunisation.
  • Non-immunological: TACO, infection risk (very low), iron overload (chronic), citrate toxicity, electrolyte disturbances.
fileciteturn0file3
Q9. Steps to minimise error in transfusion administration.
  • Strict patient ID checks, compatibility testing, bedside cross-checks, documentation, monitoring during transfusion per protocol.

30.4 Haemolytic Anaemia (SDL) (Haematology)

HaematologyMedicineEmergency Department

Case Summary

Ambrose Okeke, 25, from South Africa with sickle cell disease presents with severe left leg pain; febrile 37.8°C; prior crises requiring narcotics.

Questions & Answers

Q1. Pathophysiology of severe bone pain in SCD; anaemia, splenic sequestration and marrow aplasia mechanisms.
  • Vaso-occlusion from sickling → infarctive crises (bone, chest, CNS, spleen, liver, priapism).
  • Anaemia: shortened RBC survival (~20 days) due to haemolysis.
  • Splenic sequestration: pooling/infarction in spleen.
  • Marrow aplasia: transient suppression (e.g., parvovirus B19).
fileciteturn0file4
Q2. Pathophysiology and consequences in haemolytic anaemias.
  • RBC survival markedly shortened; marrow compensates up to 6–8× output (reticulocytosis).
  • If loss exceeds capacity → anaemia; intravascular vs extravascular haemolysis patterns.
fileciteturn0file4
Q3. Fate of haemoglobin after haemolysis (plasma/urine handling).
  • Free Hb binds haptoglobin (saturable) → liver clearance; excess filtered by kidney → haemoglobinuria; tubular reabsorption → haemosiderin; haemopexin pathway contributes.
fileciteturn0file4
Q4. Investigations to determine cause of haemolysis (flow chart summary).
  • FBC/retics (↑ if compensated); film (polychromasia, spherocytes/elliptocytes/sickle cells), bilirubin (unconjugated ↑), LDH ↑, haptoglobin ↓.
  • Urine: urobilinogen ↑; haemoglobinuria/haemosiderinuria (intravascular).
fileciteturn0file4
Q5. Blood film features in SCD with anaemia (Hb ~80 g/L, high retic).
  • Normochromic normocytic anaemia; sickle cells and target cells present.
fileciteturn0file4
Q6. Haemoglobin abnormality and pathogenesis of sickling syndromes.
  • β-globin mutation → HbS polymerisation under deoxygenation → membrane damage, rigidity, vaso-occlusion, haemolysis.
fileciteturn0file4
Q7. Principles of management of an acute painful crisis in SCD.
  • Prompt analgesia (often opioids), hydration, oxygen if hypoxic, treat triggers/infection, consider transfusion/exchange in severe complications; supportive care.
fileciteturn0file4
Q8. Long-term problems in SCD and underlying pathophysiology.
  • Chronic haemolysis/anaemia, functional asplenia, stroke, avascular necrosis, leg ulcers, pulmonary hypertension, priapism; due to recurrent vaso-occlusion and haemolysis.

30.5 Myelofibrosis (Haematology)

HaematologyMedicineHospital Ward

Case Summary

Margaret Lewis, 60, 5 months of fatigue and weight loss; splenomegaly on exam; anaemia on FBC. Haematologist suspects myeloproliferative disorder/myelofibrosis.

Questions & Answers

Q1. Key features of history/examination and why (with splenomegaly)?
  • Symptoms: anaemia (weakness, dyspnoea, dizziness), bleeding/bruising, infection/fever, jaundice, lymphadenopathy, bone pain, weight loss, paraesthesiae/skin rash.
  • Full haematological exam: nodes, hepatomegaly/ascites, testes, PR/pelvic, lungs/breast/skin (malignancy screen).
fileciteturn0file5
Q2. Spleen anatomy/function and physiological effects of hypersplenism.
  • Red pulp (sinuses/cords), white pulp (lymphoid follicles).
  • Functions: sequestration/phagocytosis of old/abnormal RBCs, extramedullary haematopoiesis, antibody production, platelet pooling.
  • Hypersplenism: pancytopenia, haemolysis, ↑ plasma volume.
fileciteturn0file5
Q3. Clinical features of myelofibrosis/hypersplenism and underlying pathophysiology.
  • Myelofibrosis: progressive marrow fibrosis (↑ PDGF), splenomegaly, extramedullary haematopoiesis (liver/spleen/LNs).
  • Insidious in >50 yrs; fatigue/weakness; abdominal fullness/early satiety; massive spleen; hepatomegaly common.
  • Progression: severe anaemia, bleeding (thrombocytopenia), perisplenitis pain, bone pain, hyperuricaemia/gout, cachexia.
fileciteturn0file5
Q4. Investigations commonly undertaken for myelofibrosis and why?
  • FBC: anaemia with leukoerythroblastic features; poikilocytes; tear-drop cells; WBC may be very high; platelets high then fall later.
  • BM aspiration often “dry tap”; core biopsy for fibrosis; consider molecular tests per specialist.
fileciteturn0file5
Q5. Post-splenectomy problems Margaret may face.
  • Overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI), thrombocytosis/thrombosis risk, vaccine-preventable infections susceptibility.
fileciteturn0file5
Q6. Ongoing GP recommendations for infection prophylaxis post-splenectomy.
  • Vaccinations (pneumococcal, Hib, meningococcal, influenza, others per schedule), consider antibiotic prophylaxis per guidelines, prompt treatment for febrile illness.

30.6 Thrombocytopaenia (Haematology)

HaematologyMedicineGeneral Practice

Case Summary

Sybilla Hanson, 35, rapidly developing leg bruising (purpura), no trauma, otherwise well. Marked bilateral purpura on exam.

Questions & Answers

Q1. Key features of history and examination and why?
  • General PMHx/FHx/social, meds/allergies.
  • Haem history: anaemia symptoms, mucosal/GI/menstrual bleeding, easy bruising/purpura, infection/fever, nodes, bone pain, weight loss.
  • Specifics: fever, pregnancy, OCP, haematuria, abdominal pain.
fileciteturn0file6
Q2. Normal haemostasis: vessel wall, platelets, coagulation, fibrinolysis — roles/functions.
  • Vessel: reflex vasoconstriction; endothelial activation.
  • Platelets: adhesion (collagen receptors), activation, degranulation; fibrinogen bridges aggregate platelets.
  • Coagulation: intrinsic/extrinsic → common pathway; thrombin converts fibrinogen → fibrin for stable plug.
  • Fibrinolysis: clot breakdown (plasmin system).
fileciteturn0file6
Q3. Investigations for a suspected bleeding disorder and rationale.
  • Repeat FBC + blood film (confirm true thrombocytopaenia; exclude CLL/MDS).
  • Renal/liver function; viral serology (EBV, hepatitis; consider HIV); autoantibodies (e.g., SLE).
  • B12/folate, immunoglobulins; clotting studies; bone marrow (secondary care) if indicated.
fileciteturn0file6
Q4. What is thrombocytopaenia and clinical effects by platelet count levels?
  • Platelets <100×10⁹/L; bleeding diathesis (reduced clotting capacity).
  • Manifestations: purpura/ecchymoses, mucosal bleeding, epistaxis, menorrhagia, PPH.
  • Severe (<30×10⁹/L): retinal/subconjunctival, GI, intracranial bleeding (rare but serious).
fileciteturn0file6
Q5. ITP definition and contrast with TTP clinical picture.
  • ITP: immune-mediated platelet destruction → isolated thrombocytopaenia; typically mucocutaneous bleeding.
  • TTP: thrombotic microangiopathy with thrombocytopaenia, MAHA, neurological/renal involvement, fever; urgent therapy.
fileciteturn0file6
Q6. Stepwise management approach for ITP.
  • Assess bleeding severity/platelet count; avoid antiplatelets/NSAIDs.
  • First-line: corticosteroids, IVIG if bleeding/severe.
  • Second-line: TPO-RAs, rituximab, splenectomy (selected cases); supportive measures and vaccination planning if splenectomy.
